Autumn 2020

Staying Organized

The first artifact I've included is my planner. It may seem a bit boring, but it represents an important accomplishment for me: staying organized. As a freshman, I've been getting a lot of advice about how to succeed in college - and planning and organization has been central to the vast majority of this advice. Keeping a planner has also pushed me to reflect on how I spend my time. How do I finish all of my assignments on time without sacrificing depth of engagement with the material? To how many activities can I commit myself? It's a balancing act that I look forward to improving as I continue on my college journey.

Community Outreach

This fall, I joined the High School Events branch of Community Outreach in the Society of Women Engineers at UW. This is the title slide of our first presentation, an event that helped high school students learn about different engineering majors. The event helped me realize that I am passionate about outreach - this is an area with which I would love to engage more. It was also a challenging moment, however, since attendance at the event was not up to our goal. Our team took this setback as an opportunity to improve our schedule for promoting events, and I know this first event will remain an inspiration for me in the future.

Face Mask

This UW logo face mask is not an artifact that is unique to me - and that is what makes it so significant. It represents my responsibility to my community, and reminds me of the recent Global Challenges discussion, which demonstrated the interdisciplinary nature of communicating about crises. Throughout the pandemic, I've had a lot of time to reflect on how we unite communities around a common goal, such as reducing the spread of coronavirus. This reflection elicits broad questions: What can we learn about communicating to the public in general from the UW's response to the pandemic? How can we continue to improve our response, and how can we apply effective communication strategies in areas outside of pandemic response?

It not only represents my first contribution to the project, but the learning process that led up to it. I learned new syntax and new concepts, and I refined my debugging strategies. Explore Engineering Conference

This artifact is a compilation of slides from the Explore Engineering Conference, an event I did with SWE High School Events. It was an event for which I had a big role - we had 16 volunteer presenters for the event, and, as the Speaker Coordinator, I was responsible for finding and communicating with these presenters. It was inspiring to see the result of all my work and planning throughout the quarter, especially since students were involved and excited to ask questions. At the start of the year, I never would have thought I could hold so much responsibility for such a large event, or that I could accomplish what I did this quarter. But I did, and now I am encouraged to get even more involved.

Grades, Learning, Problem-Solving

This is a section of the point totals for a problem set I did in 312. It was the worst grade I had received on an assignment thus far. Though I was initially disappointed, I remembered all the advice students and professors had given me: a bad grade is a learning experience, not a reflection of who you are as a person. The grade was a reminder that my main focus was learning and understanding the material, not just completing assignments and worrying about point totals. I focused more on preparing and studying for problem sets and went to office hours for the first time in the quarter. Though it was stressful, I'm glad that I had this experience - the quarter overall was challenging, but it was the one in which I grew the most and it was the most rewarding.


Experiential Learning: Storage Management Research

This quarter, I participated in my first experiential learning activity. I worked with a graduate student in the UW Database Group on TASM, a tile-based storage manager for video analytics. Essentially, it provides a faster way to store and access videos that are labeled with object information by splitting videos into tiles (spatial subsets) based on the location of objects within the frames.
